Precalculus for Environmental Science: Foundations for Modeling

Chapter 5 Quadratic Functions & Optimization

5.1 Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  • Identify the vertex of a quadratic function and interpret it in context.
  • Complete the square to convert between forms of a quadratic.
  • Graph a parabola from its equation.
  • Set up and solve optimization problems using quadratic functions.

5.2 Quadratic Form & the Vertex

A quadratic function has the standard form

\[ f(x) = ax^2 + bx + c \]

Its graph is a parabola — it opens upward if \(a>0\) and downward if \(a<0\). The single highest or lowest point on the parabola is called the vertex, and in vertex form:

\[ f(x) = a(x-h)^2 + k \]

the vertex is simply \((h, k)\) — you can read it directly off the equation. This is the quadratic equivalent of what point-slope form was for lines: the same function, written in a form that makes one particular feature (here, the vertex) immediately visible.

For a quadratic in standard form, the vertex’s \(x\)-coordinate is:

\[ x = -\frac{b}{2a} \]

Why a Maximum, Not a Minimum?

A parabola modeling crop yield as a function of fertilizer amount opens downward. Before doing any algebra, what does that tell you about what happens if you apply either too little or too much fertilizer?

5.3 Completing the Square

Completing the square converts standard form into vertex form. The core idea: take the \(x^2\) and \(x\) terms, and add and subtract the exact amount needed to make them a perfect square trinomial.

5.3.1 Worked Example: Converting a Yield Model

Suppose crop yield (in bushels/acre) as a function of fertilizer applied (\(x\), in kg/acre) is modeled by:

\[ Y(x) = -2x^2 + 40x + 50 \]

Step 1 — Factor \(a\) out of the first two terms.

\[ Y(x) = -2(x^2 - 20x) + 50 \]

Step 2 — Complete the square inside the parentheses. Take half of \(-20\), square it: \((-10)^2 = 100\). Add and subtract it inside:

\[ Y(x) = -2(x^2 - 20x + 100 - 100) + 50 = -2\big[(x-10)^2 - 100\big] + 50 \]

Step 3 — Distribute the \(-2\) and simplify.

\[ Y(x) = -2(x-10)^2 + 200 + 50 = -2(x-10)^2 + 250 \]

Step 4 — Read off the vertex and interpret. Vertex: \((10, 250)\). Since \(a = -2 < 0\), this is a maximum: applying 10 kg/acre of fertilizer is predicted to maximize yield at 250 bushels/acre. Applying more or less than 10 kg/acre — in either direction — is predicted to reduce yield.

Concept Check: The Vertex Formula

1. Find the vertex of \(f(x) = 3x^2 - 12x + 7\) using the vertex formula, without completing the square.

Click to reveal answer

\(x = -\dfrac{-12}{2(3)} = 2\). Then \(f(2) = 3(4)-12(2)+7 = 12-24+7=-5\). Vertex: \((2,-5)\).

2. A quadratic has \(a > 0\). Is its vertex a maximum or a minimum, and how do you know from the shape of the graph?

Click to reveal answer

Minimum. When \(a>0\), the parabola opens upward, like a bowl — the vertex sits at the bottom, the lowest point on the graph.

5.4 Graphing Parabolas

Once you know the vertex and the sign of \(a\), you can sketch a parabola quickly: plot the vertex, note whether it opens up or down, and use the \(y\)-intercept (\(f(0) = c\)) as a second point to gauge the width.

5.5 Optimization Problems

Many environmental questions boil down to finding a maximum or minimum of some quantity — the highest sustainable harvest, the lowest-cost containment design, the peak of a pollutant plume. When the relationship is quadratic, the vertex is the answer to the optimization question.

5.5.1 Worked Example: Minimizing the Cost of a Containment Berm

An engineer needs to build a rectangular containment berm with a fixed perimeter of 200 m around a storage site, and wants to choose the dimensions that maximize the enclosed area (to contain as much runoff as possible for the same amount of berm material).

Step 1 — Set up variables. Let \(x\) = one side length. Since the perimeter is fixed at 200 m, the two pairs of sides satisfy \(2x + 2y = 200\), so \(y = 100 - x\).

Step 2 — Write area as a function of one variable.

\[ A(x) = x \cdot y = x(100-x) = -x^2 + 100x \]

Step 3 — Find the vertex.

\[ x = -\frac{100}{2(-1)} = 50 \]

Step 4 — Solve for the other dimension and interpret. \(y = 100 - 50 = 50\). The area-maximizing berm is a \(50\text{ m} \times 50\text{ m}\) square, enclosing \(A(50) = 50(50) = 2500\) m² — the largest possible area for that fixed amount of perimeter material.

Concept Check: Interpreting Max/Min in Context

1. In the berm example, why does setting \(y = 100 - x\) let us write area as a function of \(x\) alone?

Click to reveal answer

Because the perimeter constraint ties \(x\) and \(y\) together — once \(x\) is chosen, \(y\) is forced to be \(100-x\) to keep the perimeter fixed at 200. Substituting removes the second variable, leaving a single-variable quadratic we can optimize with the vertex formula.

2. If the optimization problem asked to minimize cost instead of maximize area, would you still look for the vertex?

Click to reveal answer

Yes — the vertex is always the extreme point of a parabola, whether that extreme is a maximum or a minimum depends on the sign of \(a\), not on which type of problem you’re solving. A cost function that opens upward (\(a>0\)) has its vertex as a minimum, which is exactly what a cost-minimization problem is looking for.

5.6 Practice: Optimization Case Study

5.6.1 What Are You Practicing?

Turning a verbal optimization scenario into a quadratic function of a single variable, then using the vertex to answer the original question.

5.6.2 How to Approach These Problems

  1. Identify the quantity being optimized (this becomes your output, e.g. area, cost, yield).
  2. Identify the constraint that relates your variables (a fixed perimeter, a fixed budget, a fixed total).
  3. Use the constraint to eliminate one variable, leaving a single-variable quadratic.
  4. Find the vertex and interpret both coordinates in context.

5.6.3 Practice Problems

1. A conservation group has \($6000\) to spend fencing a rectangular wildlife exclosure, where fencing costs \($30\) per meter of perimeter. They want to maximize enclosed area. Set up the area function and find the optimal dimensions.

Click to reveal solution

Total perimeter affordable: \(6000/30 = 200\) m, same setup as the berm example: \(2x+2y=200 \Rightarrow y=100-x\), \(A(x) = -x^2+100x\), vertex at \(x=50\), so \(y=50\) — a 50 m × 50 m square, enclosing 2500 m².

2. The height of a projected water fountain arc at a restored wetland is modeled by \(h(t) = -5t^2 + 20t\), where \(h\) is height in meters and \(t\) is time in seconds. Find the maximum height and the time at which it occurs.

Click to reveal solution

Vertex: \(t = -\dfrac{20}{2(-5)} = 2\). \(h(2) = -5(4)+20(2) = -20+40=20\). Maximum height of 20 m at \(t=2\) seconds.

5.7.2 Vertex-Finding

Use the vertex formula to find the vertex of each function.

  1. \(f(x) = x^2 - 4x + 1\)
  2. \(f(x) = -3x^2 + 12x - 2\)
  3. \(f(x) = 0.5x^2 - 2x + 6\)
