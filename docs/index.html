Precalculus for Environmental Science: Foundations for Modeling

Precalculus for Environmental Science: Foundations for Modeling

Gaj Sivandran

2026-07-03

Chapter 1 Why Precalculus?

A biologist notices that a lake’s oxygen level swings up and down with the seasons, peaking every summer and bottoming out every winter. A hydrologist watches a contaminant plume spread outward from a spill site in a widening circle. A climate scientist fits a curve to fifty years of temperature data to project what next decade might look like.

None of these questions can be answered with arithmetic alone. They require a language for describing shapes of change—lines, circles, curves, cycles—and the tools to translate a real system into an equation you can actually work with.

That language is precalculus.

Welcome to Precalculus for Environmental Science.

This book is your on-ramp to the Environmental Calculus sequence. It is not a re-run of your high school math class. Every tool you build here—coordinate systems, functions, exponentials, trigonometry—is chosen because you will use it, directly, to model a real environmental system in the chapters and courses that follow.

1.1 Why This Book?

Most precalculus textbooks are written to prepare students for any future math course, so their examples tend to be abstract: generic curves, unlabeled axes, “find x.” That approach works, but it misses an opportunity—precalculus concepts map beautifully onto environmental systems, and concepts stick best when they’re connected to something you care about.

In this book, every major idea is anchored in an environmental context:

  • Lines model steady trends—rising sea level, a linear dose-response relationship.
  • Circles describe buffer zones, contamination radii, and orbital paths.
  • Exponentials and logarithms govern decay, growth, and scales like pH.
  • Trigonometry and sinusoids capture cycles—tides, seasons, daylight hours.

Our goal isn’t just to get you ready for the next exam. It’s to help you see precalculus as a toolkit you’ll reach for constantly as an environmental scientist.

1.2 What Is Precalculus, Really?

At its core, precalculus is the study of relationships between quantities—and the vocabulary for describing how those relationships behave. Here are a few examples you’ll encounter in this book:

System The Relationship The Precalculus Tool
Atmosphere CO₂ concentration rising roughly steadily over decades Linear functions
Toxicology A drug or pollutant concentration decaying over time Exponential functions
Water chemistry Acidity spanning many orders of magnitude Logarithmic scales
Tides & seasons A quantity that rises and falls in a repeating cycle Sinusoidal functions
Contamination The boundary of an affected area around a point source Circles & coordinate geometry

Notice the pattern: each real system has a shape, and each shape has a matching mathematical family. Learning to recognize which family fits a given system—and then finding the specific equation—is the central skill of this course.

Shapes of Change

Think of an environmental quantity you’ve read about recently (temperature, a pollutant level, a population count, tide height). In a small group, discuss: does it change steadily, does it grow or shrink multiplicatively, or does it repeat in cycles? What shape would its graph have?

Concept Check: Matching Systems to Math

See if you can match each scenario to the type of function that best describes it.

1. A tank drains so that the volume of water decreases by the same number of liters every minute. Linear, exponential, or sinusoidal?

Click to reveal answer

Linear. A constant rate of change (same amount lost every minute) is the hallmark of a linear relationship.

2. A radioactive tracer’s activity drops by half every 8 days. Linear, exponential, or sinusoidal?

Click to reveal answer

Exponential. A fixed fraction lost per time interval (a “half-life”) is the signature of exponential decay.

3. The number of daylight hours in Seattle rises and falls smoothly over the course of a year, repeating annually. Linear, exponential, or sinusoidal?

Click to reveal answer

Sinusoidal. A repeating, cyclical pattern is best captured by a sine or cosine function.

1.3 How This Book Works

Each chapter follows the same supportive structure:

Explanations with environmental context. New ideas are introduced in plain language first, then formalized, always tied to a real environmental system.

Prompt boxes are places to pause and think—some ask you to discuss with a partner, some ask you to sketch or write, some pose a question to sit with before reading on:

Example Prompt

This is where you’ll find a question or activity. The write-in space below is for your notes.

Concept checks appear throughout each chapter—low-stakes, click-to-reveal questions so you can immediately test whether an idea has clicked.

Worked examples walk through environmental problems step by step: what we’re doing, why, and what the answer means in context.

Skills drills at the end of each chapter give you pure mechanical repetition—no context, just reps—to build fluency with the algebra itself.

Workbook chapters (added in a later phase of this book) will follow each content chapter, giving you guided practice, write-in prompts, and additional problems.

1.4 Success in This Class

First and foremost, know this: we believe in your ability to learn and grow. You bring unique strengths and perspectives, and your effort matters here.

If precalculus material feels rusty or unfamiliar, that’s completely normal—it doesn’t mean you don’t belong in environmental science. Mistakes are how you find out where your thinking needs refinement, and that’s exactly where growth happens.

Just like fieldwork, learning math is iterative: try, test, revise, improve. Give yourself permission to be imperfect while you build these tools—you’ll need them, and you’re capable of mastering them.

1.5 Looking Ahead

By the end of this book, you will be able to:

  • Track units and coordinates through environmental calculations and check results for reasonableness
  • Build and interpret linear, quadratic, exponential, logarithmic, rational, and sinusoidal models from real environmental data
  • Compose and invert functions to move between related environmental quantities
  • Use trigonometry and circular motion to describe cyclical environmental processes
  • Read, graph, and transform functions to communicate environmental trends with mathematical precision

These are exactly the tools you’ll need on day one of Environmental Calculus.

This book is a companion volume to Environmental Calculus, written to prepare students for that course using the same environmental-science lens. It is a work in progress—expect rough edges, and expect them to get smoothed out with your help.
