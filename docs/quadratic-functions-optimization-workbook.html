Precalculus for Environmental Science: Foundations for Modeling

Chapter 16 Quadratic Functions & Optimization — Workbook

16.1 Learning Objectives (Quick Self-Check)

Check your readiness: For each objective, write one sentence in your own words or a question you still have:

  1. How do I find the vertex of a quadratic function?
  2. How do I complete the square?
  3. How do I set up and solve an optimization problem using a quadratic?
Notes:

16.2 Vertex Form & Completing the Square

16.2.1 Instructor Demo: A Different Yield Model

A crop’s yield model is \(Y(x) = -3x^2 + 36x + 10\), where \(x\) is fertilizer (kg/acre).

(Instructor walks through this live: complete the square to convert to vertex form, then interpret the vertex.)

16.2.2 Your Turn: Vertex Form & Completing the Square

1. Find the vertex of \(f(x) = x^2 - 8x + 3\) using the vertex formula.

2. Find the vertex of \(f(x) = -2x^2 + 16x - 10\) using the vertex formula.

3. Complete the square to write \(f(x) = x^2 + 10x + 21\) in vertex form.

4. Complete the square to write \(f(x) = -4x^2 + 16x + 5\) in vertex form.

5. A population model \(P(t) = -0.5t^2 + 10t + 100\) predicts population as a function of years since a habitat restoration began. Find the vertex and interpret both coordinates.

6. Sketch (by hand or describe in words) the graph of \(f(x) = 2(x-3)^2 - 8\): where is the vertex, does it open up or down, and what is the y-intercept?

Notes:

16.3 Optimization Problems

16.3.1 Instructor Demo: A Fixed-Budget Buffer Strip

A farmer has enough native grass seed to plant a buffer strip with a fixed perimeter of 300 m along a field edge, and wants to maximize the enclosed area to trap the most runoff.

(Instructor walks through this live: set up the constraint, write area as a function of one variable, and find the vertex.)

16.3.2 Your Turn: Optimization Problems

1. A rectangular fenced plot has a perimeter of 400 m. Find the dimensions that maximize enclosed area.

2. A ball’s height is modeled by \(h(t) = -5t^2+30t\), \(h\) in meters, \(t\) in seconds. Find the maximum height and when it occurs.

3. A city has $9000 budgeted for fencing a rectangular community garden at $45/meter of perimeter. Find the dimensions that maximize the enclosed area.

4. A projectile’s trajectory is modeled by \(h(x) = -0.02x^2 + 2x\), where \(x\) is horizontal distance (m) and \(h\) is height (m). Find the maximum height and the horizontal distance at which it occurs.

5. A fish hatchery release program predicts return numbers as \(R(x) = -2x^2+80x\), where \(x\) is thousands of juveniles released. Find the release amount that maximizes returns, and the maximum predicted return.

6. A cost function for a water treatment process is \(C(x) = 0.5x^2 - 40x + 1200\), where \(x\) is treatment rate (thousand gallons/day). Find the rate that minimizes cost, and the minimum cost.

Notes:

16.4 Challenge

16.4.1 Harder Versions

1. A rectangular exclosure will be built along an existing straight riverbank, so fencing is only needed on three sides (two widths and one length), with 240 m of fencing available. Set up the area function and find the dimensions that maximize enclosed area. (This is different from the earlier four-sided perimeter problems — set up your constraint carefully.)

2. A quadratic cost function has vertex \((20, 500)\) and passes through the point \((0, 900)\). Find the full equation of the quadratic in standard form. (Hint: start from vertex form with unknown \(a\), then use the given point to solve for \(a\).)

Notes:

16.4.2 Environmental & Biological Applications

1. A predator-prey model predicts prey population as \(N(x) = -4x^2 + 120x\), where \(x\) is the number of predators present. At what predator population is prey population maximized, and what does it mean ecologically that prey population decreases for predator populations beyond that point (rather than continuing to rise)?

2. A wetland’s water filtration efficiency (in %) as a function of plant density \(x\) (plants/m²) is modeled by \(E(x) = -0.8x^2 + 24x\). Find the plant density that maximizes filtration efficiency, and the maximum efficiency achieved. Is a plant density of 0 or a very high plant density better or worse than this optimum, and why might overly dense planting actually reduce efficiency in a real wetland?

Notes:

Answer Key

Vertex Form & Completing the Square — Your Turn

  1. \(x=4\), \(f(4)=16-32+3=-13\): vertex \((4,-13)\)
  2. \(x=4\), \(f(4)=-32+64-10=22\): vertex \((4,22)\)
  3. \(f(x) = (x+5)^2 - 4\)
  4. \(f(x) = -4(x-2)^2 + 21\)
  5. Vertex: \(t=-\dfrac{10}{2(-0.5)}=10\); \(P(10)=-50+100+100=150\). At 10 years post-restoration, population peaks at 150.
  6. Vertex \((3,-8)\), opens upward (\(a=2>0\)); y-intercept: \(f(0)=2(9)-8=10\)

Optimization Problems — Your Turn

  1. \(2x+2y=400 \Rightarrow y=200-x\); \(A(x)=x(200-x)=-x^2+200x\); vertex at \(x=100\), so \(y=100\): a 100 m × 100 m square.
  2. Vertex: \(t=-\dfrac{30}{2(-5)}=3\); \(h(3)=-45+90=45\) m at \(t=3\) s.
  3. Perimeter budget: \(9000/45=200\) m; same setup: \(x=y=50\), a 50 m × 50 m square.
  4. Vertex: \(x=-\dfrac{2}{2(-0.02)}=50\); \(h(50)=-0.02(2500)+100=50\) m at \(x=50\) m.
  5. Vertex: \(x=-\dfrac{80}{2(-2)}=20\); \(R(20)=-800+1600=800\); release 20,000 juveniles for a maximum predicted return of 800 (thousand, consistent with the model’s units).
  6. Vertex: \(x=-\dfrac{-40}{2(0.5)}=40\); \(C(40)=0.5(1600)-1600+1200=800-1600+1200=400\); minimum cost of 400 at a treatment rate of 40 (thousand gal/day).

Challenge — Harder Versions

  1. Let \(x\) = the two widths, \(y\) = the one length: \(2x+y=240 \Rightarrow y=240-2x\). \(A(x)=x(240-2x)=-2x^2+240x\). Vertex: \(x=-\dfrac{240}{2(-2)}=60\); \(y=240-120=120\). Dimensions: 60 m (each width) × 120 m (length), enclosing \(60\times120=7200\) m².
  2. Vertex form: \(f(x)=a(x-20)^2+500\). Using \((0,900)\): \(900=a(400)+500 \Rightarrow 400a=400 \Rightarrow a=1\). So \(f(x)=(x-20)^2+500 = x^2-40x+400+500=x^2-40x+900\).

Challenge — Environmental & Biological Applications

  1. Vertex: \(x=-\dfrac{120}{2(-4)}=15\); prey maximized at 15 predators. Beyond 15 predators, the model predicts prey decline — ecologically, more predators eventually means more predation pressure than the prey population can sustain, overwhelming whatever earlier benefit (e.g., controlling a different competing pressure) the model assumes at lower predator densities.
  2. Vertex: \(x=-\dfrac{24}{2(-0.8)}=15\); \(E(15)=-0.8(225)+360=-180+360=180\)… this exceeds 100%, which is a reminder that this simplified model shouldn’t be trusted far from realistic plant densities — but within the model, plant density of 15/m² maximizes efficiency. Both very low density (too little filtration surface) and very high density (likely overcrowding, competition for light/nutrients, or flow blockage in a real wetland) reduce efficiency below this peak, which is exactly the downward-opening parabola shape.
