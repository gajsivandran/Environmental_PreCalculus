Precalculus for Environmental Science: Foundations for Modeling

Chapter 3 Lines, Circles & Linear Modeling

3.1 Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  • Write and interpret the equation of a line in slope-intercept and point-slope form.
  • Write and interpret the equation of a circle.
  • Fit a linear model to environmental data and interpret its slope and intercept.
  • Solve for the intersection of two lines and interpret the result in context.
  • Model uniform linear motion using parametric equations.

3.2 Equations of Lines

3.2.1 Slope-Intercept and Point-Slope Form

A line can be written in slope-intercept form:

\[y = mx + b\]

where \(m\) is the slope (the rate of change of \(y\) with respect to \(x\)) and \(b\) is the y-intercept (the value of \(y\) when \(x = 0\)).

If instead you know one point \((x_1, y_1)\) on the line and the slope \(m\), it’s often faster to use point-slope form:

\[y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)\]

Both forms describe the exact same line — point-slope form is just a shortcut for building the equation before simplifying it into slope-intercept form.

Slope as a Rate

Recall the Upstream Well station from Chapter 1. Suppose its nitrate concentration was 6.0 mg/L in year 0 and 9.6 mg/L in year 4. What are the “rise” and “run” here, in words, before you compute anything?

3.2.2 Worked Example: Writing a Trend Line

Using the Upstream Well data above — 6.0 mg/L at year 0, 9.6 mg/L at year 4:

Step 1 — Compute the slope.

\[ m = \frac{9.6 - 6.0}{4 - 0} = \frac{3.6}{4} = 0.9 \ \frac{\text{mg/L}}{\text{yr}} \]

Step 2 — Use point-slope form with \((0, 6.0)\).

\[ y - 6.0 = 0.9(x - 0) \]

Step 3 — Simplify to slope-intercept form.

\[ y = 0.9x + 6.0 \]

Step 4 — Interpret. Nitrate at this well is rising by about 0.9 mg/L per year, starting from a baseline of 6.0 mg/L. If this linear trend continues, we can predict future concentrations — which is exactly what we’ll do in the next section.

3.3 Circles

3.3.1 The Equation of a Circle

A circle is the set of all points at a fixed distance (the radius, \(r\)) from a fixed point (the center, \((h,k)\)). Since the distance formula from Chapter 1 gives the distance between any point \((x,y)\) and the center, setting that distance equal to \(r\) — and squaring both sides to clear the square root — gives the equation of a circle:

\[ (x-h)^2 + (y-k)^2 = r^2 \]

3.3.2 Worked Example: A Contamination Buffer Zone

Suppose a chemical spill occurs at the Downstream Well, located at \((180, -40)\) in our watershed’s local coordinate grid (meters east/north of the stream gauge). Regulators establish a 150 m buffer zone around the spill site where groundwater sampling is required.

Step 1 — Identify center and radius. Center: \((180, -40)\). Radius: \(150\) m.

Step 2 — Write the equation.

\[ (x - 180)^2 + (y + 40)^2 = 150^2 = 22500 \]

Step 3 — Use it. Is the Wetland Inlet, at \((60, 150)\), inside the buffer zone? Compute the distance from the spill site to the inlet and compare it to 150 m:

\[ d = \sqrt{(60-180)^2 + (150-(-40))^2} = \sqrt{(-120)^2 + 190^2} = \sqrt{14400+36100} = \sqrt{50500} \approx 224.7 \text{ m} \]

Since \(224.7 > 150\), the Wetland Inlet sits outside the buffer zone.

Concept Check: Circle Equations

1. What is the center and radius of the circle \((x-3)^2 + (y+5)^2 = 49\)?

Click to reveal answer

Center \((3, -5)\), radius \(\sqrt{49} = 7\). Note the sign flips: the equation subtracts the center’s coordinates, so \((y+5)^2\) means \(k = -5\).

2. A monitoring buffer has center \((0,0)\) and radius 300 m. Is the point \((200, 220)\) inside, outside, or on the buffer boundary?

Click to reveal answer

Distance from origin: \(\sqrt{200^2+220^2} = \sqrt{40000+48400} = \sqrt{88400} \approx 297.3\) m, which is just inside the 300 m buffer.

3. Why does the equation of a circle involve squared terms, while the equation of a line does not?

Click to reveal answer

A circle is defined by a fixed distance from a center, and distance itself involves a square root of squared differences (Chapter 1). Squaring both sides to remove the square root is what introduces the squared terms. A line, by contrast, is defined by a constant rate of change, which is a first-degree (unsquared) relationship between \(x\) and \(y\).

3.4 Linear Modeling from Data

Real data rarely falls exactly on a line — but if the underlying process is roughly linear, we can find the line that best fits the scatter of points. This is called linear regression, and while the full statistical method is beyond this course, the result is something you already know how to use: a slope-intercept equation you can evaluate and interpret.

Figure 3.1: A simulated multi-decade CO2 trend with a fitted linear model.

3.4.1 Worked Example: Interpreting a Fitted Line

Suppose fitting a line to the data above gives:

\[ C(t) = 1.8t + 336.4 \]

where \(t\) is years since 1980 and \(C(t)\) is CO₂ concentration in ppm.

  • Slope (1.8): CO₂ is rising by about 1.8 ppm per year, on average, over this period.
  • Intercept (336.4): The model predicts a concentration of 336.4 ppm at \(t=0\), i.e., in 1980.
  • Prediction: In 2030 (\(t = 50\)), the model predicts \(C(50) = 1.8(50)+336.4 = 426.4\) ppm.

Trusting a Linear Model

A linear model fit to 40 years of data is used to predict a value 50 years into the future. What could go wrong with that kind of extrapolation? Discuss with a partner.

3.5 Intersections of Lines

Two lines intersect at the point (or points) where they share the same \(x\) and \(y\) values simultaneously — in other words, where their equations are both true at once. Setting the two right-hand sides equal to each other and solving for \(x\) gives the intersection.

3.5.1 Worked Example: When Do Two Trends Cross?

Suppose nitrate at the Upstream Well follows \(y = 0.9x + 6.0\) (from our earlier example), while nitrate at the Downstream Well follows \(y = 0.4x + 9.0\), where \(x\) is years since monitoring began.

Step 1 — Set the equations equal.

\[ 0.9x + 6.0 = 0.4x + 9.0 \]

Step 2 — Solve for \(x\).

\[ 0.5x = 3.0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad x = 6 \]

Step 3 — Solve for \(y\) using either equation.

\[ y = 0.9(6) + 6.0 = 11.4 \]

Step 4 — Interpret. According to these two linear models, the Upstream and Downstream Wells are predicted to have the same nitrate concentration (11.4 mg/L) 6 years after monitoring began — even though they started at different levels and are changing at different rates.

Concept Check: Interpreting Slope

1. Two contamination trend lines have the same slope but different intercepts. Will they ever intersect?

Click to reveal answer

No. Equal slopes mean the lines are parallel — they change at the same rate and never converge or cross, since the vertical gap between them (the difference in intercepts) stays constant forever.

2. In the worked example above, which well’s nitrate level is rising faster, and how do you know?

Click to reveal answer

The Upstream Well, since its slope (0.9) is larger than the Downstream Well’s slope (0.4) — it’s gaining mg/L per year faster, even though it started lower.

3.6 Uniform Linear Motion & Parametric Equations

So far, \(x\) and \(y\) have been related directly to each other. But sometimes it’s more natural to describe both \(x\) and \(y\) as functions of a third variable — usually time, \(t\). This is a parametric equation.

For an object moving at a constant velocity in a straight line, its position at time \(t\) is:

\[ x(t) = x_0 + v_x t, \qquad y(t) = y_0 + v_y t \]

where \((x_0, y_0)\) is the starting position and \((v_x, v_y)\) is the constant velocity in each direction.

3.6.1 Worked Example: Do Two Plumes Meet?

Two contaminant plumes are each modeled as a point moving through groundwater at constant velocity. Plume A starts at \((-100, 0)\) and moves at \((v_x, v_y) = (15, 5)\) m/year. Plume B starts at \((100, 100)\) and moves at \((v_x, v_y) = (-10, 0)\) m/year.

Step 1 — Write parametric equations for each plume.

\[ \text{A:}\quad x_A(t) = -100 + 15t, \quad y_A(t) = 0 + 5t \] \[ \text{B:}\quad x_B(t) = 100 - 10t, \quad y_B(t) = 100 \]

Step 2 — Set the x-coordinates equal and solve for \(t\).

\[ -100 + 15t = 100 - 10t \quad \Rightarrow \quad 25t = 200 \quad \Rightarrow \quad t = 8 \]

Step 3 — Check whether the y-coordinates also match at that \(t\).

\[ y_A(8) = 5(8) = 40, \qquad y_B(8) = 100 \]

Step 4 — Interpret. Since \(40 \neq 100\), the two plumes are at the same x-position at \(t=8\) years but at different y-positions — they pass each other without meeting. This is an important distinction: matching one coordinate is not enough to say two moving objects collide; both coordinates must match at the same time.

3.7 Practice: Fit-a-Line Case Study

3.7.1 What Are You Practicing?

These problems combine writing line equations, fitting a trend, finding intersections, and reasoning about parametric motion — all applied to watershed and monitoring scenarios.

3.7.2 How to Approach These Problems

  1. Identify what kind of relationship you’re modeling — a static line, a circle, or motion over time.
  2. Write down what’s given (points, slopes, rates) before choosing an equation form.
  3. Solve algebraically, then interpret the result in the original context.

3.7.3 Practice Problems

1. A sensor’s calibration follows a linear relationship. At a true concentration of 0 mg/L, it reads 0.2 mg/L. At a true concentration of 10 mg/L, it reads 9.7 mg/L. Find the line relating true concentration (\(x\)) to sensor reading (\(y\)).

Click to reveal solution

Slope: \(m = \dfrac{9.7-0.2}{10-0} = 0.95\). Using point-slope with \((0, 0.2)\): \(y - 0.2 = 0.95(x-0)\), so \(y = 0.95x + 0.2\).

2. A restricted-access zone is a circle centered at \((50, 50)\) with radius 80 m. Write its equation, and determine whether the point \((100, 120)\) falls inside it.

Click to reveal solution

Equation: \((x-50)^2+(y-50)^2 = 6400\). Distance from center to point: \(\sqrt{50^2+70^2} = \sqrt{2500+4900}=\sqrt{7400}\approx 86.0\) m, which is outside the 80 m radius.

3. Two population models predict deer counts in a preserve: Model A: \(y = 12x + 200\), Model B: \(y = 8x + 260\), where \(x\) is years from now. In how many years do the models predict the same population, and what is it?

Click to reveal solution

\(12x+200 = 8x+260 \Rightarrow 4x = 60 \Rightarrow x = 15\). Population: \(y = 12(15)+200 = 380\).

4. A drifting buoy starts at \((0, 200)\) and moves at \((v_x, v_y) = (20, -5)\) m/hr. Write its parametric equations and find its position after 6 hours.

Click to reveal solution

\(x(t) = 20t\), \(y(t) = 200 - 5t\). At \(t=6\): \(x = 120\), \(y = 170\), so the buoy is at \((120, 170)\).

3.8 Skills Drills

3.8.1 Writing Line Equations

Write the slope-intercept equation of the line through each pair of points.

  1. \((0,2)\) and \((4,10)\)
  2. \((1,5)\) and \((3,1)\)
  3. \((-2,0)\) and \((0,6)\)
  4. \((2,7)\) and \((2,-3)\) — what goes wrong here, and why?
  5. \((0,-4)\) and \((5,-4)\)
Click to reveal answers
  1. \(m=2\), \(y = 2x+2\)
  2. \(m=-2\), \(y=-2x+7\)
  3. \(m=3\), \(y=3x+6\)
  4. Both points share \(x=2\), so the “line” is vertical (\(x=2\)) — it has no slope-intercept form because the slope is undefined (division by zero when computing rise/run).
  5. \(m=0\), \(y=-4\) (a horizontal line)

3.8.2 Solving Intersections Algebraically

Find the intersection point of each pair of lines.

  1. \(y = 3x+1\) and \(y = -2x+11\)
  2. \(y = 5x-4\) and \(y = 5x+2\)
  3. \(y = x\) and \(y = -x+8\)
  4. \(y = 0.5x+3\) and \(y = 2x-6\)
