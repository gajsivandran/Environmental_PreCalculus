Precalculus for Environmental Science: Foundations for Modeling

Chapter 14 Lines, Circles & Linear Modeling — Workbook

14.1 Learning Objectives (Quick Self-Check)

Check your readiness: For each objective, write one sentence in your own words or a question you still have:

  1. How do I write and interpret the equation of a line?
  2. How do I write and interpret the equation of a circle?
  3. How do I fit and interpret a linear model, and find where two lines intersect?
  4. How do I describe motion at constant velocity using parametric equations?
Notes:

14.2 Lines & Slope

14.2.1 Instructor Demo: A Streamflow Trend

A stream gauge reads 12 m³/s in year 0 and 18 m³/s in year 6.

(Instructor walks through this live: compute the slope, write the equation in point-slope form, simplify to slope-intercept form, and interpret both the slope and intercept in context.)

14.2.2 Your Turn: Writing & Interpreting Lines

1. A well’s water level was 22 m below the surface in year 0 and 16 m below the surface in year 8. Write the line describing depth as a function of year, and interpret the slope.

2. A utility’s per-unit electricity cost is $0.12/kWh with a flat $15 monthly fee. Write a cost function \(C(x)\) for \(x\) kWh used.

3. Given the line \(y = -2x + 40\) modeling a pollutant’s concentration (mg/L) over time (years), what does the negative slope tell you about the pollutant, and when does the model predict a concentration of zero?

4. Find the equation of the line through \((2, 9)\) and \((6, 1)\).

5. A glacier’s terminus retreats from an elevation marker at a rate of 15 m/year, starting at 0 m in year 0. Write the line describing retreat distance vs. year, and predict the retreat after 12 years.

6. Two competing linear cost estimates for a restoration project are \(C_1(x) = 500x + 2000\) and \(C_2(x) = 650x + 500\), where \(x\) is acres restored. Which has the higher starting cost? Which has the higher per-acre cost?

Notes:

14.3 Circles & Buffer Zones

14.3.1 Instructor Demo: A New Spill Buffer

A chemical spill occurs at \((50, -30)\) on a local grid. Regulators set a 200 m buffer zone.

(Instructor walks through this live: write the circle’s equation, then check whether a specific nearby station falls inside or outside the buffer using the distance formula.)

14.3.2 Your Turn: Circle Equations & Buffer Checks

1. Write the equation of a circle centered at \((10, -5)\) with radius 60.

2. A monitoring buffer has equation \((x-20)^2+(y+10)^2 = 2500\). What are its center and radius?

3. A 100 m buffer is centered at \((0,0)\). Is the point \((70, 80)\) inside, outside, or on the boundary?

4. A restricted zone has center \((100, 100)\) and radius 150. Is a station at \((200, 50)\) inside the zone?

5. Write the equation of a circle with center \((-15, 25)\) that passes through the point \((-15, 40)\). (Hint: first find the radius using the distance between the center and the given point.)

6. Two spill buffers are centered at \((0,0)\) with radius 80, and at \((120, 0)\) with radius 60. Do the two buffer zones overlap? (Hint: compare the distance between centers to the sum of the radii.)

Notes:

14.4 Modeling, Intersections & Parametric Motion

14.4.1 Instructor Demo: When Do Two Trends Cross?

Two contaminant sources have trend lines \(y = 1.2x + 4\) and \(y = 0.6x + 10\), where \(x\) is years.

(Instructor walks through this live: set the equations equal, solve for the intersection, and interpret what the crossing point means for the two sources.)

14.4.2 Your Turn: Modeling, Intersections & Motion

1. Find the intersection of \(y = 3x+2\) and \(y = -x+18\).

2. Two population models are \(y=25x+300\) (Model A) and \(y=40x+150\) (Model B), \(x\) in years. When do they predict equal populations?

3. A buoy starts at \((0, 50)\) and drifts at \((v_x, v_y) = (8, -3)\) m/hr. Write its parametric equations and find its position after 5 hours.

4. A contaminant plume starts at \((-200, 0)\) moving at \((12, 4)\) m/yr. Write its parametric equations, and find its position after 10 years.

5. Two drifting buoys: Buoy A starts at \((0,0)\) moving at \((10, 6)\) m/hr; Buoy B starts at \((100, 0)\) moving at \((-5, 6)\) m/hr. At what time \(t\) do their x-coordinates match? Do their y-coordinates also match at that time?

6. A river gauge reads 5 ppm at year 0 and 8 ppm at year 4 for one pollutant, while a second pollutant reads 12 ppm at year 0 and decreases by 0.5 ppm/year. In what year (if any) are the two pollutant levels equal?

Notes:

14.5 Challenge

14.5.1 Harder Versions

1. A circular buffer zone of radius \(r\) is centered at the origin. A straight monitoring transect follows the line \(y = x\). At what point(s) does the transect cross the buffer boundary, in terms of \(r\)? (Hint: substitute \(y=x\) into the circle equation and solve for \(x\).)

2. Three sensors report linear trends: \(y=2x+5\), \(y=-x+20\), and \(y=0.5x+10\). Find all three pairwise intersection points, and determine whether all three lines pass through a single common point.

Notes:

14.5.2 Environmental & Biological Applications

1. Two predator territories are modeled as circular buffers: Territory A, radius 4 km centered at \((0,0)\); Territory B, radius 3 km centered at \((6, 0)\). Do the territories overlap? If so, by how much do the boundaries overlap along the line connecting the two centers? (Hint: compare the distance between centers to the sum and difference of the radii.)

2. A fish population in a hatchery-supplemented stream is modeled by \(P(t) = 40t + 500\) (with supplementation) and, if supplementation stopped, would instead follow \(P(t) = -15t + 500\), where \(t\) is years. In what year would the two scenarios predict the same population (other than \(t=0\), where they start equal)? What does your answer tell you about whether the two lines actually diverge over time?

Notes:

Answer Key

Click to reveal Your Turn and Challenge answers

Lines & Slope — Your Turn

  1. Slope \(= \dfrac{16-22}{8-0} = -0.75\); \(y = -0.75x+22\). The well’s water level is rising (depth below surface decreasing) by 0.75 m/year.
  2. \(C(x) = 0.12x + 15\)
  3. The concentration is decreasing by 2 mg/L per year. Zero when \(0 = -2x+40 \Rightarrow x=20\) years.
  4. Slope \(=\dfrac{1-9}{6-2}=-2\); \(y-9=-2(x-2) \Rightarrow y=-2x+13\)
  5. \(y = 15x\); at \(x=12\): \(y=180\) m
  6. \(C_1\) has the higher starting cost ($2000 vs $500); \(C_2\) has the higher per-acre cost ($650 vs $500)

Circles & Buffer Zones — Your Turn

  1. \((x-10)^2+(y+5)^2=3600\)
  2. Center \((20,-10)\), radius \(50\)
  3. \(\sqrt{70^2+80^2}=\sqrt{4900+6400}=\sqrt{11300}\approx106.3\), outside the 100 m buffer
  4. \(\sqrt{100^2+50^2}=\sqrt{10000+2500}=\sqrt{12500}\approx111.8 < 150\), inside the zone
  5. Radius \(=\sqrt{0^2+15^2}=15\); equation: \((x+15)^2+(y-25)^2=225\)
  6. Distance between centers \(=120\); sum of radii \(=80+60=140\). Since \(120<140\), the circles overlap.

Modeling, Intersections & Motion — Your Turn

  1. \(3x+2=-x+18 \Rightarrow x=4, y=14\): \((4,14)\)
  2. \(25x+300=40x+150 \Rightarrow 15x=150 \Rightarrow x=10\) years
  3. \(x(t)=8t\), \(y(t)=50-3t\); at \(t=5\): \((40, 35)\)
  4. \(x(t)=-200+12t\), \(y(t)=4t\); at \(t=10\): \((-80, 40)\)
  5. \(10t = 100-5t \Rightarrow 15t=100 \Rightarrow t=6.67\) hr. y-coordinates: \(y_A=6(6.67)=40\), \(y_B=6(6.67)=40\) — yes, they match (both move at the same \(v_y=6\), so if x matches, y automatically matches too).
  6. \(5+2x = 12-0.5x \Rightarrow 2.5x=7 \Rightarrow x=2.8\) years

Challenge — Harder Versions

  1. Substituting \(y=x\): \(x^2+x^2=r^2 \Rightarrow 2x^2=r^2 \Rightarrow x=\pm\dfrac{r}{\sqrt2}\). Crossing points: \(\left(\dfrac{r}{\sqrt2},\dfrac{r}{\sqrt2}\right)\) and \(\left(-\dfrac{r}{\sqrt2},-\dfrac{r}{\sqrt2}\right)\).
  2. Line 1 & 2: \(2x+5=-x+20 \Rightarrow x=5,y=15\). Line 1 & 3: \(2x+5=0.5x+10 \Rightarrow x=3.33,y=11.67\). Line 2 & 3: \(-x+20=0.5x+10\Rightarrow x=6.67,y=13.33\). The three intersection points are all different, so the three lines do not share a single common point.

Challenge — Environmental & Biological Applications

  1. Distance between centers \(=6\). Sum of radii \(=7\), difference \(=1\). Since \(1<6<7\), the circles overlap partially (neither separate nor one fully inside the other).
  2. \(40t+500 = -15t+500 \Rightarrow 55t=0 \Rightarrow t=0\). The only equal point is \(t=0\) itself — for any \(t>0\) the two scenarios diverge immediately (one increasing, one decreasing), so supplementation clearly changes the long-run trajectory rather than just delaying an inevitable crossing.
